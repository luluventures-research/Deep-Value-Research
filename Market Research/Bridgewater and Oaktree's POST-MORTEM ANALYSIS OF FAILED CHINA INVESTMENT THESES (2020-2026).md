### POST-MORTEM ANALYSIS OF FAILED CHINA INVESTMENT THESES (2020-2026)

**TO:**  The Risk Management Committee  **FROM:**  Office of the Senior Portfolio Strategist  **DATE:**  March 15, 2026  **SUBJECT:**  Post-Mortem Analysis of Oaktree Capital and Bridgewater Associates' Investment Losses in China and Implications for Country-Specific Risk Frameworks

#### 1.0 Introduction: A Paradigm Shift in China's Investability

This memorandum dissects the significant investment losses incurred in China by two of the world's most sophisticated asset managers, Oaktree Capital Management and Bridgewater Associates. The purpose is not to assign blame but to derive actionable lessons that must inform the evolution of our firm’s risk framework. The events of 2020-2026 signal a fundamental, structural shift in China's investment landscape, challenging the long-held "Convergence Thesis"—the belief that China's markets would inevitably mature and align with Western norms.The two cases serve as definitive bookends for this regime change. Oaktree Capital’s failure represents the misapplication of a time-tested distressed debt playbook—a bet on tangible collateral—that proved fatally flawed when confronted with the priorities of a state-capitalist system. Bridgewater Associates’ failure was the collapse of a macro-ideological thesis—a bet on the inevitability of historical cycles and China’s rise—that could not withstand the force of geopolitics and sovereign policy shifts.By examining these failures, this analysis will inform a necessary recalibration of our own approach to China. We must move beyond passive, long-term allocation models and develop a more nuanced understanding of sovereign risk, political primacy, and the fundamental structure of the market itself.

#### 2.0 Deconstruction of the Macro-Strategic Environment (2020-2026)

The investment failures of Oaktree and Bridgewater were not primarily the result of firm-specific analytical errors. Rather, they were symptoms of a structural reformation of the Chinese market that rendered traditional investment models obsolete. To understand why their strategies failed, we must first analyze the macro-environmental forces that created this new, hostile regime.

##### 2.1 The "Three Red Lines" Policy Shock

The primary catalyst for the crisis was the August 2020 introduction of the "Three Red Lines" policy. This regulatory directive was explicitly designed to de-leverage China's real estate sector, which had grown to account for nearly a third of the country's GDP. By imposing strict limits on developer debt-to-asset, debt-to-equity, and cash-to-short-term-debt ratios, Beijing abruptly halted the liquidity cycle that both developers and their foreign creditors depended upon. For decades, investors viewed high-yield developer debt as a secure bet backed by tangible assets. They failed to recognize that the value of those assets was entirely dependent on a continuous flow of credit, which the state could, and did, turn off overnight.

##### 2.2 The Onset of a Balance Sheet Recession

By 2024, the policy-induced liquidity crisis had metastasized into a classic balance sheet recession. With property prices collapsing by approximately 40% in tier-one cities, two critical dynamics emerged that invalidated core investment theses:

* **Consumer Capitulation:**  The psychological shock of falling property values and unfinished homes crippled consumer confidence. This invalidated the "consumption upgrade" thesis that underpinned many equity investments, including Bridgewater’s positions in e-commerce giants like Alibaba and JD.com.  
* **The Liquidity Trap:**  Despite government stimulus and record-low interest rates, credit demand vanished. Households and corporations prioritized repaying debt over new investment. This created a hostile environment for distressed debt investors like Oaktree, as there was no functioning market or "bid" for the restructured assets they sought to recover.

##### 2.3 The Geopolitical Risk Premium

Layered on top of the domestic economic crisis was a sharp deterioration in US-China relations. The "Trump Trade" dynamics of 2025, marked by renewed tariff threats and investment restrictions, fundamentally re-rated Chinese equities. Assets like Alibaba ceased to be valued solely on business fundamentals and instead became subject to "listing risk" and "custody risk." The existential threat of forced delisting or sanctions became a primary driver of valuation, compelling investors like Bridgewater to liquidate positions regardless of their long-term view on the underlying business.These structural changes—a policy-driven credit shock, a subsequent balance sheet recession, and a non-negotiable geopolitical risk premium—created the traps into which Oaktree and Bridgewater fell.

#### 3.0 Case Study I: Oaktree Capital and the Failure of the Distressed Debt Thesis

This section analyzes how Oaktree Capital's time-tested "loan-to-own" strategy, predicated on the security of tangible collateral and the enforceability of legal contracts, failed catastrophically when applied to China's structural property crisis.

##### 3.1 The Investment Thesis: Applying a Western Playbook

Oaktree’s core strategy was to extend secured loans to distressed entities, specifically China Evergrande Group, with the assumption that legal enforcement over high-quality collateral provided a definitive floor to value. Their exposure was approximately $1 billion in secured loans collateralized by two "crown jewel" assets: "Project Venice" on the mainland and "Project Castle" in Hong Kong. The logic was simple: if Evergrande defaulted, Oaktree would seize the assets at a deep discount and monetize them, believing they were buying dollar assets for 50 cents.

##### 3.2 "Project Venice" – The Operational Quagmire

The "Project Venice" case is a stark example of a legal victory translating to a profound economic defeat. Following Evergrande's default, Oaktree successfully seized legal control of the massive resort development. However, they quickly discovered that seizing the asset meant inheriting the state-mandated liability of completing pre-sold homes to maintain social stability. Oaktree could not liquidate the land or auction the project because local authorities prioritized preventing social unrest over honoring foreign creditor rights. This transformed Oaktree’s role from a secured creditor to an unpaid state contractor, forced to operate at a loss in a market with no viable exit.

##### 3.3 "Project Castle" – The Valuation Wipeout

The "Project Castle" asset in Hong Kong demonstrates that even a common law, offshore jurisdiction provided no protection from systemic value destruction. Receivers appointed by Oaktree successfully sold the undeveloped land for approximately  $637 million. While this price covered Oaktree's \~$ 520 million loan principal, it represented a catastrophic failure of the investment mandate. Evergrande had invested over $1.1 billion into the project, and the sale price was nearly $390 million lower than what Evergrande had attempted to sell it for just months prior. For a distressed investor targeting high rates of return, merely recovering principal after a protracted and risky process signifies a significant opportunity cost and a breakdown of the core investment thesis.

##### 3.4 Synthesis of Failure: Misjudging Systemic Risk

Oaktree's failure can be distilled into three core misjudgments, the systemic nature of which was underscored by similar deep-loss exits by peers. This value destruction was not unique to Oaktree, as firms like Carlyle and BlackRock were simultaneously forced into similar disposals in the Shanghai commercial market.

* **Legal Enforcement Mirage:**  Oaktree conflated  *legal*  control (the ability to seize a project's equity) with  *economic*  control (the ability to monetize the asset). In China's state-capitalist system, economic control ultimately rests with the Party, which dictates outcomes based on its own priorities.  
* **Illiquidity of Collateral:**  The "loan-to-own" thesis is fundamentally dependent on a functioning secondary market where seized assets can be sold. The "Three Red Lines" policy intentionally eliminated this market, rendering Oaktree's collateral illiquid and effectively worthless as a near-term source of recovery.  
* **Underestimation of the Sovereign Put:**  Oaktree incorrectly assumed the Chinese state would ultimately backstop the property market to protect the system. Instead, Beijing chose to let the bubble burst, deliberately imposing losses on foreign investors to curb moral hazard and restructure the economy.Oaktree's mechanical failure to enforce its rights over tangible assets stands in contrast to Bridgewater's more ideological collapse.

#### 4.0 Case Study II: Bridgewater Associates and the Capitulation of the "China Bull" Thesis

Bridgewater's losses represent an ideological failure rooted in founder Ray Dalio’s "Changing World Order" thesis, which posited China’s inevitable rise as a global power. The firm’s strategic liquidation of its entire US-listed China portfolio in 2025 marked a definitive and public capitulation of this long-held conviction.

##### 4.1 The Investment Thesis: The Inevitability of a Rising Empire

Bridgewater's strategy was to allocate heavily to "New Economy" Chinese equities—such as Alibaba, JD.com, and PDD—as a strategic diversification away from stagnating Western markets. Guided by a belief in China's inexorable growth, the firm demonstrated high conviction by actively "buying the dip" throughout the regulatory crackdowns of 2021 and 2022, viewing them as temporary adjustments rather than a permanent regime change.

##### 4.2 The Forced Liquidation: The Q2 2025 Exit

In the second quarter of 2025, Bridgewater executed a stunning reversal, selling all of its holdings in 16 US-listed Chinese companies, valued at approximately  $1.5 billion. The sale included cornerstone positions, such as \~5.66 million shares of Alibaba and \~2.79 million shares of JD.com. The timing and completeness of the exit, particularly the decision to sell at \~$ 118 per share for Alibaba only to miss a subsequent relief rally into the  $140-$ 150 range, provides irrefutable evidence that this was not a tactical market call. It was a strategic, forced liquidation, likely compelled by breaches of internal risk limits or overwhelming client pressure related to geopolitical exposure.

##### 4.3 The Onshore vs. Offshore Paradox

The most critical insight from the Bridgewater case is the stark divergence between its offshore and onshore strategies. While its US-listed equity portfolio was collapsing, its China-domiciled fund was delivering exceptional returns. This divergence proves that successful investing in China is no longer about betting on the growth of its private-sector corporations. Instead, it requires aligning with the state's macro-management objectives—trading the bond market as the government manages a deflationary slowdown and trading commodities as it manages strategic priorities. Bridgewater failed in its bet on Chinese capitalism but succeeded in its bet on the Chinese state's ability to control its domestic economy.The symbolic capstone to this ideological shift was the transition of power at Bridgewater itself. In 2025, Ray Dalio sold his remaining stake in the firm. Under a new CEO, Nir Bar Dea, the firm executed a "strategic overhaul," moving away from its founder's worldview. The liquidation of the ADR portfolio was the final severing of the Dalio-era conviction, marking the definitive end of Bridgewater’s ideological China bull thesis.

#### 5.0 Synthesized Lessons and Implications for Risk Frameworks

This section synthesizes the Oaktree and Bridgewater post-mortems into a coherent set of strategic conclusions that must inform the evolution of our firm's country-specific risk models for China. The old playbook is broken, and a new one must be written.

##### 5.1 The End of "Blind Allocation"

The era of making broad-based, passive allocations to themes like "China Growth" or "China Distressed" is definitively over. Oaktree’s experience demonstrates that the value of a physical asset is not determined by its legal title but by local political priorities and social stability concerns. Bridgewater’s experience shows that macroeconomic growth has become decoupled from the corporate profitability and shareholder returns of its largest companies. Any future allocation must be surgical, tactical, and deeply informed by policy direction, not broad economic narratives.

##### 5.2 The Primacy of Sovereign Policy over Legal Contracts

The Oaktree "Project Venice" case must be internalized as a core principle of our risk assessment: in a systemic crisis, the Chinese state will prioritize social stability and Party objectives over the contractual rights of foreign creditors. The concept of "rule of law" as understood in Western markets is subordinate to the "rule of the Party." This political risk is not a tail risk; it is a central feature of the market that must be explicitly modeled and priced into any investment. Legal enforceability cannot be assumed to equate to economic recovery.

##### 5.3 The Decoupling is a Portfolio Reality

Bridgewater’s total liquidation of its US-listed Chinese equity portfolio is the ultimate confirmation that geopolitical risk has moved from a theoretical concept to a primary driver of capital allocation. The risk that an asset becomes "uninvestable" due to sanctions, capital controls, or forced delisting can now completely supersede its fundamental valuation. For US-domiciled fiduciaries, holding these assets now carries a non-diversifiable political risk that may be unacceptable at any price.

##### 5.4 The Specter of "Japanification" without the Rebound

The deflationary pressures, vanished credit demand, and \~40% collapse in property values strongly suggest a "Japanification" scenario for China. However, a critical distinction exists that fundamentally alters the risk calculus for foreign capital. While Japan's "lost decade" eventually presented a generational value opportunity for investors who could wait for the recovery, Bridgewater’s strategic exit demonstrates that China’s assets may not offer the same prospect. The hostile geopolitical overlay means that even if Chinese assets become fundamentally cheap, they may remain "uninvestable" for US fiduciaries due to the overriding risks of sanctions and delisting. This raises the prospect of a lost decade that is potentially permanent for offshore investors, who may be structurally barred from participating in any eventual rebound.In conclusion, the investment paradigm for China has fundamentally shifted. It is no longer a "buy and hold" asset class geared for long-term compounding, but a tactical trading environment defined by extreme volatility and the necessity of sovereign alignment. This requires nothing less than a fundamental overhaul of our risk assessment tools and capital allocation frameworks.
