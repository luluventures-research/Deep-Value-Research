## **Latest Quarter Earnings Summary (Q4 2025 & FY2025)**

Alphabet (Google’s parent) delivered robust Q4 2025 results, capping a milestone year . Q4 revenue was $113.8 billion, up 18% YoY (17% in constant currency) . Full-year 2025 revenue reached $402.8 billion, topping $400 billion for the first time (15% growth YoY) . Growth was broad-based: Google Services (ads, Android, YouTube, etc.) rose 14% to $95.9 billion in Q4, led by Search ads (+17%) and YouTube ads (+9%) . Google Cloud revenue surged 48% to $17.7 billion in Q4 as enterprises adopted its AI infrastructure . Quarterly operating income was $35.9 billion (32% margin), up 16% despite a $2.1 billion Waymo employee stock expense . Net income jumped 30% to $34.5 billion in Q4 (EPS $2.82) , and full-year 2025 net profit was $132.2 billion (EPS $10.81) – a 32% YoY increase . Alphabet’s profitability expanded with FY2025 net margin \~32.8%, aided by cost controls and other income gains (e.g. investment gains boosted “Other Income” to $29.8 billion ). CEO Sundar Pichai highlighted that YouTube’s annual revenue exceeded $60 billion and Google Cloud reached a $70 billion annual run-rate by year-end . He also noted Alphabet’s heavy AI investments are paying off in usage (Gemini AI model launch) and will continue – the company announced an unprecedented $175–185 billion CapEx plan for 2026 to meet AI-driven demand . Notably, the board approved Alphabet’s first-ever cash dividend in 2024 and continued hefty buybacks . Overall, Q4 and 2025 results underscore Alphabet’s accelerating growth in core ad businesses and Google Cloud, translating into record revenue and earnings .

## **Business Model**

Alphabet’s Business Model is predominantly powered by technology platforms that attract billions of users, monetized mainly via advertising. Google, its largest division, operates products like Search, YouTube, Android, Chrome, Maps, Gmail, Google Play, and more . Advertising is the primary income source – Google’s search ads and YouTube ads together contributed about 72% of Q4 2025 revenue (Search & other $63.1B; YouTube ads $11.4B; Google Network ads $7.8B) . Google “Other” revenues (Play Store apps, cloud storage, hardware, YouTube subscriptions, etc.) add \~12% . The fast-growing Google Cloud segment (15–16% of revenue) sells cloud infrastructure & software services to enterprises . Other Bets (Waymo, Verily, etc.) are nascent businesses contributing under 1%. Alphabet’s revenue mix is illustrated below:

Customers & Scale: Alphabet’s platforms serve billions of users globally. Google Search processes over 5 billion queries per day , commanding \~92% search market share . YouTube has 2+ billion monthly logged-in users and over 1 billion hours watched per day . Google Cloud now has a 13% market share in cloud (2025) , and counts 9 of the top 10 AI labs (e.g. OpenAI, Anthropic) as clients . Alphabet also reported over 325 million paid subscriptions across YouTube Premium, YouTube TV, Google One, etc. – reflecting a growing base of paying users. The sheer user scale and rich data from these services form a feedback loop attracting millions of advertisers (Google had an estimated millions of advertising customers, from small businesses to Fortune 500, seeking access to its user base).

Moat & Competitive Advantages: Alphabet is widely regarded as a “wide-moat” company . Key moat sources are: Network effects & Scale – Google’s search engine improves as more users search and more advertisers bid, reinforcing its \~92% global search share . This dominance yields outsized profits and barriers to entry. Technological edge & IP – Google’s proprietary search algorithms and AI research (e.g. PageRank, TensorFlow, TPUs) underpin superior search quality and ad targeting . Data & AI Flywheel – billions of user interactions across Google’s products provide data to train better AI models (like Google’s Gemini AI launched in 2025\) and enhance services, which attracts more usage and further data – a self-reinforcing advantage in the AI era . Brand & Trust – “Google” is synonymous with search (to “Google” something) – a strong brand built on reliability and user trust . While users might switch if a clearly better option arose, Google’s continuous innovation (e.g. integrating AI into search) helps maintain its mindshare and user loyalty . Ecosystem lock-in – Products like Android (with \~70% smartphone OS share), Chrome (browser), Gmail, Maps, and YouTube create an integrated ecosystem, increasing switching costs for users. Advertisers are effectively locked-in by Google’s unparalleled reach and ROI on ad spend. Economies of scale – Alphabet’s massive infrastructure (data centers, fiber networks, undersea cables) and custom silicon (TPU AI chips) allow it to deliver services at a cost per user that smaller rivals can’t match. All these factors create formidable “alligators in Google’s moat” that Buffett and others have noted . As of 2025, Alphabet’s competitive advantages appear intact or even widening in fields like generative AI .

## **Headwinds & Risks**

Short-Term (temporary) headwinds: Alphabet faces some near-term challenges mostly tied to macroeconomic and policy factors. A potential global economic slowdown or recession could soften advertising spending, directly impacting Google’s core ads revenue (which is cyclical). For instance, during early 2023’s weaker economy, Google’s ad growth slowed markedly. Currency fluctuations are another short-term factor – a strong US dollar can reduce reported international revenue (Alphabet often cites “constant currency” growth differences ). Rising costs in areas like talent and energy can pinch margins in the short run; Alphabet has responded with cost controls (e.g. a January 2023 layoff of 12,000 staff ). Regulatory news flow is another source of volatility: for example, ongoing antitrust trials by the DOJ (on Google’s search distribution deals) introduce uncertainty and negative headlines in the near term . However, these processes unfold slowly. Investor sentiment can also swing on short-term results – in Q4 2025, despite strong earnings, Alphabet’s stock dipped on news of a big 2026 spending plan , reflecting concern that heavy CapEx might dent near-term free cash flow. Such knee-jerk market reactions are usually short-lived if core fundamentals remain strong. In sum, temporary headwinds include the ad cycle (tied to economic conditions), currency effects, short-term cost inflation, and regulatory noise – none of which permanently impair Alphabet’s business, but they can cause a few bumpy quarters.

Long-Term (structural) risks: Alphabet’s greatest challenges are structural shifts and regulation that could erode its dominance over a decade-long horizon. A primary risk is technological disruption in search – specifically the rise of AI chatbots and intelligent assistants that bypass traditional search. Generative AI (like OpenAI’s ChatGPT and others) can answer questions directly, potentially reducing the need to “Google” things . This threatens Alphabet’s search query volumes, ad impressions, and lucrative high-intent searches over time . The risk isn’t an overnight collapse but a “decade-long erosion” of the search-centric model if AI-based interfaces gain favor . Alphabet is investing heavily to integrate AI into its own products (e.g. Bard, Gemini) to mitigate this, but must balance innovation with protecting its ad business . Another structural risk is regulatory pressure and antitrust. Alphabet’s dominance in search, Android (mobile OS), browser (Chrome), and ad tech has invited regulatory scrutiny globally. Regulators in the EU and U.S. are considering or implementing measures: e.g. banning default search deals (like Google’s \~$15 billion/yr payment to Apple for being iPhone’s default), requiring Android to offer rival search options, limiting how Google can tie together its ad services, or even breaking up parts of the company . Such actions, if enforced, could permanently impact business by making it harder for Google to acquire traffic or leverage integration advantages, thereby weakening its moat . Privacy regulations (like the EU’s GDPR or cookie deprecation) also pose a long-term risk by limiting data for ad targeting, potentially compressing ad margins. Additionally, competition from other tech giants is intensifying: Microsoft (with its OpenAI partnership) is embedding AI in Windows/Office/Bing to chip at Google’s user habits ; Apple’s control of iOS could increasingly favor its own services over Google’s; Amazon competes for ad budgets (in e-commerce search) and cloud clients; Meta (Facebook) is pushing AI and has enormous social media engagement that could divert ad spend. While none of these rivals may “dethrone” Alphabet outright, collectively they can slow growth and squeeze margins over time . Lastly, geopolitical factors could permanently impact segments of Alphabet’s business – for example, in China (which blocks Google services) or if international data-sharing rules fragment the internet (splinternet scenario), Google’s global reach could be curtailed. Summarily, the biggest long-term risks are (1) technological shifts (AI) reducing reliance on Google’s search, (2) regulatory interventions altering its business practices or structure , and (3) evolving competitive landscape (AI-empowered rivals and platform owners challenging its core businesses) . These are slow-moving but potentially profound risks that investors must monitor over the next decade.

## **Cost Structure**

Alphabet’s cost structure comprises both high fixed costs (huge infrastructure & R\&D investments) and variable costs that scale with usage or revenue. Cost of revenue was $162.5 billion in 2025 (40.3% of revenue) . Key components of cost of revenue include Traffic Acquisition Costs (TAC) – the fees paid to partners like Apple, Mozilla, carriers, etc., to make Google the default search or to distribute Google ads. TAC in Q4 2025 was $16.6 billion (about 23% of Google ad revenues) . These payments scale directly with search and ad revenue – a largely variable cost. Another big variable portion is content acquisition costs (e.g. the revenue share paid out to YouTube creators and app developers). Additionally, running Google’s services incurs massive infrastructure costs (data center electricity, network bandwidth, server depreciation). While infrastructure investments are fixed upfront, the depreciation and maintenance scale with capacity usage – somewhat semi-variable. Alphabet’s other operating expenses reflect heavy fixed costs in innovation and platform growth: in 2025, it spent $61.1 billion on R\&D (15% of revenue) and $28.7 billion on Sales & Marketing (7%) . R\&D includes salaries for tens of thousands of engineers (Alphabet had 190,800 employees as of Q4 2025\) , and is largely fixed in the short-run (projects like AI research or self-driving cars incur huge upfront costs regardless of quarterly revenue). Sales & Marketing includes advertising for Google’s own hardware/services and the costs of sales teams (e.g. Google Cloud sales reps); these can be trimmed in tough times but generally scale slower than revenue (S\&M was up only 3% YoY in 2025\) . General & Administrative (G\&A) was $21.5 billion in 2025 (5.3% of revenue) , containing corporate overhead, legal fees, and fines. G\&A jumped \~51% in 2025 – possibly due to legal reserves and the $2.1 billion one-time stock compensation charge at Waymo . This category has fixed elements (e.g. management salaries) but also unpredictable spikes (regulatory fines or legal settlements).

Cost per unit: Given Google mostly offers digital products “free” to users, a meaningful unit cost is hard to pinpoint. One could consider cost per search query or per ad served, which is very low (fractions of a cent) thanks to scale. For Google Cloud, the cost per customer decreases with scale as data center utilization rises – its gross margin turned positive recently, reflecting improved unit economics. On hardware (Pixel phones, etc.), there is a traditional cost of goods per unit, but hardware remains a small slice of Alphabet.

Labor expenses: Alphabet is known for high compensation – median salary was nearly $300,000 in 2021 . Total employee costs (wages, stock-based compensation, benefits) are substantial. Stock-based comp was \~$24.9 billion in 2025 (which, while non-cash, dilutes shareholders unless offset by buybacks). The company slowed hiring in 2023–2024 (even laid off \~6% of staff in 2023\) after a headcount surge in prior years . Still, labor (especially engineers for R\&D) remains a large fixed cost, as cutting too deeply could harm innovation.

Advertising & Marketing spend: Alphabet doesn’t need to advertise Google Search, but it does market Cloud services, consumer devices (Pixel, Nest), and YouTube subscriptions. S\&M expense was \~$28.7 billion in 2025 . This includes everything from cloud conference sponsorships to app store promotions. Marketing spend tends to rise when new products launch (e.g. Pixel phone ads) but can be scaled back if needed (these are more discretionary than R\&D).

Most significant costs: The largest expense by far is Cost of Revenues ($162.5B) , which includes TAC and infrastructure. Within that, TAC alone is \~$56 billion annually (estimated, given Q4 $16.6B ) – essentially a revenue-share to partners. The second biggest is R\&D ($61B), reflecting Alphabet’s innovation-centric strategy. These two dwarf other costs. Sales & marketing and G\&A are an order of magnitude smaller.

Trends & economic sensitivity: In boom times, Google’s revenue can outpace expenses – e.g. 2021 revenue jumped 41%, and operating margin expanded to 35% . In leaner times (like 2022’s slowdown), revenue growth fell to 10% while costs kept growing, squeezing operating margin to 25% . Alphabet has shown ability to adjust: after investor pressure in 2022 to cut costs , it reduced workforce and office footprint charges in 2023\. Variable costs (TAC, content payouts) move with revenue, providing some natural buffer – if ad revenue drops, TAC drops proportionally. Fixed costs like R\&D are harder to trim quickly; however, management did moderate R\&D growth in 2022–2023. During economic downturns (e.g. 2008, 2020), Alphabet tends to slow hiring, cut travel/marketing budgets, and focus on core projects. Conversely, in expansion phases it invests aggressively in new bets (as seen in heavy CapEx plans for AI). Over the long run, economies of scale have improved profitability – e.g. Google’s operating margin hovered \~25–27% a decade ago and is \~30%+ now , despite massive R\&D, indicating cost growth has been slower than revenue growth. Summarily, traffic acquisition and infrastructure costs scale with usage, labor and R\&D are largely fixed (but subject to management’s strategic scaling), and marketing is semi-discretionary. Alphabet’s high gross margin (\~60%) and operating margin (\~30%) in 2025 reflect a business that, while spending lavishly on innovation, benefits from tremendous scale economies.

## **Management & Governance**

Executive Team: Alphabet is led by Sundar Pichai (CEO of Alphabet & Google), who has been with Google since 2004 and became Google CEO in 2015 (Alphabet CEO in 2019). Pichai is known for his low-key leadership and product focus. The long-time CFO Ruth Porat (joined 2015 from Morgan Stanley) was promoted in 2023 to President & Chief Investment Officer of Alphabet, handing CFO duties to Anat Ashkenazi (formerly Eli Lilly CFO) in July 2024 . Other key execs include Kent Walker (Chief Legal Officer), Prabhakar Raghavan (SVP Search), Philipp Schindler (Chief Business Officer for ads), and Thomas Kurian (CEO Google Cloud). Notably, Larry Page and Sergey Brin – Google’s co-founders – are no longer involved in daily operations (they stepped down from CEO/President roles in 2019), but they remain on Alphabet’s Board and retain majority voting control via super-voting shares . Alphabet’s Board of Directors (10 members) is chaired by former Stanford President John Hennessy . Besides Page, Brin, Pichai, and Hennessy, the board includes independent directors like Frances Arnold (Nobel-winning scientist), Roger W. Ferguson Jr. (ex-CEO of TIAA), Marty Chávez (ex-Goldman Sachs CIO), Robin Washington (former Gilead CFO), Ann Mather (former Pixar CFO), and VC John Doerr . Historically, Eric Schmidt (CEO 2001–2011, Executive Chair to 2017\) was a key leader who guided Google’s early growth; he left the board in 2019\. Overall, Alphabet’s leadership blends company veterans (Pichai, Brin, Page) with experienced outsiders (Porat, Ashkenazi, independent directors).

Past Performance & Integrity: Alphabet’s management has generally delivered on its strategic goals. Under Pichai, Google navigated the shift to mobile and cloud successfully, and he set “AI-first” direction that led to world-leading AI capabilities. Management has a record of meeting or exceeding financial targets (Alphabet consistently beats analyst expectations on revenue/EPS in recent years ). They are also known to invest for the long term (sometimes to Wall Street’s frustration). There have been instances of less transparency – e.g. for years Google didn’t disclose YouTube or Cloud profitability, only beginning to break those out in 2020\. However, this improved transparency eventually (Cloud’s operating profit is now reported, etc.). There is little evidence of management “hiding problems” from investors; that said, communication could be better – on earnings calls, Google’s answers can be vague, focusing on long-term vision rather than granular details (analysts sometimes push for more color on, say, Cloud margins or YouTube trends). One notable controversy was the Google+ data leak (2018) which management initially kept quiet – not for financial reasons but to avoid reputational damage; this decision drew criticism for lack of candor. In terms of integrity, Google’s board faced a shareholder lawsuit over handling of sexual misconduct by executives (e.g. large payouts to departing execs accused of harassment) . That suit settled in 2020 with Alphabet pledging changes in governance. So, while not a financial integrity issue, it was a lapse in upholding “Don’t be evil” principles internally. No major financial fraud or accounting issues have emerged – Alphabet’s reporting is regarded as clean by auditors. Analyst relations: Alphabet’s tone on calls is generally modest and avoids hype. Sundar Pichai and Porat answer questions directly but strategically. They don’t provide formal revenue guidance, which some investors find frustrating, but this practice is consistent.

Management’s tenure is long relative to peers: Pichai \~19 years at Google, many SVPs have 10–20+ years at the firm (indicative of stability in the ranks). The board, too, includes longtime insiders (Page, Brin, Doerr since 1999, Mather since 2005). There have been questions of governance due to the dual-class share structure giving founders 51% voting power with only \~6% economic interest . This insulates management from activist investor pressures – sometimes a positive for long-term strategy, but a risk if management goes astray. Shareholder proposals to eliminate unequal voting rights have been consistently defeated by the founders’ votes . Some shareholders and proxy advisors critique this structure as entrenchment.

Value Alignment: Alphabet’s leadership is financially aligned with shareholders to a degree, but not perfectly. The co-founders Page and Brin certainly have the vast majority of their wealth in Alphabet stock (each holding \~3% of Alphabet’s shares but controlling \~26% voting power each , worth \~$120 billion each). Thus they are highly motivated to increase long-term share value. Sundar Pichai’s compensation includes large stock grants that vest on performance; for example, he received a \~$200 million stock award in 2019 and another in 2022, largely tied to Alphabet’s total shareholder return. His net worth (several hundred million dollars) is mostly in Alphabet stock. So Pichai will indeed “earn more from growing the business” via stock appreciation than from his base salary (which is relatively modest \~$2 million). Other top execs like Porat have also accumulated significant Alphabet shares and typically see stock grants as the bulk of compensation. One indicator of alignment: insider selling – Google’s execs do regularly sell some shares (often through automatic 10b5-1 plans) for diversification, but there’s no sign of dumping or lack of confidence. We haven’t seen executives making opportunistic buys on the open market during dips – but this is common in big tech (they rely on stock grants rather than buying). Importantly, share repurchases by the company (authorized $70 billion annually in recent years) show management prefers returning capital via buybacks rather than dividends, which can signal they believe the stock is undervalued and that reinvesting in their own shares is a good use of capital . The recent introduction of a small dividend in 2024 was more to broaden the investor base than because insiders wanted cash – since founders and many execs are so wealthy, they benefit more if the cash were plowed back into growth or buybacks. Overall, Alphabet’s management appears long-term oriented and skin-in-the-game, though the dual-class stock means they can make decisions even if outside shareholders disagree. So far, that hasn’t led to value-destructive moves – they have generally deployed capital rationally and addressed issues (like cost cuts in 2023\) when pushed.

## **Shareholders & Capital Returns**

Top Shareholders: Due to Alphabet’s dual-class structure (Class A GOOGL with 1 vote/share, Class B held by insiders with 10 votes/share, and Class C GOOG with no votes), the co-founders Larry Page and Sergey Brin effectively control 51% of voting power with a relatively small equity stake (\~6% combined) . Each of them holds about 3% of Alphabet’s total shares but their Class B super-votes give them outsized control. Besides the founders, the largest shareholders are institutional investors. As of mid-2025, The Vanguard Group was the biggest, holding \~7.7% of Alphabet’s capital (across A and C shares) . BlackRock was next (\~6.5%), and State Street about 3.5% . Other major holders include Fidelity (\~2%), Capital Group (\~1.4%), T. Rowe Price (\~1.7%), and Morgan Stanley (\~1.3%) . Among individuals, no one comes close to Page/Brin; however, notable is John Doerr (board member) who holds a significant stake via VC firm Kleiner Perkins (Doerr was an early investor – he’s listed as a top individual owner in some sources ). In summary, Alphabet’s ownership is roughly: \~14% held by founders/insiders, \~86% by public shareholders (mostly index funds and institutional investors) .

Voting & Governance Issues: With founders in control, external shareholders have limited ability to influence governance via voting. For example, at the 2025 annual meeting, a proposal to establish equal voting rights for all shares (one-share-one-vote) received 3.83 billion votes in favor vs 8.66 billion against – defeated essentially by the insider votes . Large institutions did support it (that 3.83B in favor likely represents most non-insider votes), indicating top institutional holders favor governance reform, but Page/Brin vetoed it. Other shareholder proposals – on topics like human rights, AI ethics, climate goals, and audit of content risks – were similarly voted down in 2025 . Notably, 15 stockholder proposals were on the 2025 ballot (ranging from asking Alphabet to report on charitable contributions to assessing discrimination in AI) and none passed . The only items routinely approved are management’s slate of directors and auditor ratification. However, big institutional holders have been vocal: In late 2022, UK hedge fund TCI (Chris Hohn), holding a \~$6 billion stake , sent letters urging Alphabet to cut costs and reduce headcount by 20% to improve efficiency . This external pressure likely contributed to Alphabet’s decision to lay off 12,000 employees in January 2023 . So while formal votes don’t restrain management, shareholder sentiment (especially from respected investors) can sway management decisions. For instance, in 2023 Alphabet also agreed to more AI oversight and transparency after some shareholder and employee pressure (an AI ethics proposal was on the ballot, though it didn’t pass, management preemptively formed an AI ethics review process).

At annual meetings, top voted topics recently have been governance and social responsibility. For example, proposals in 2025 included: reporting on board diversity and non-discrimination in AI systems, enhanced whistleblower protections, and evaluating political lobbying alignment with climate goals . Each major institutional holder has its own stance: Vanguard and BlackRock often vote with management on routine issues but have supported some ESG proposals. For instance, a proposal on “shareholder right to call special meetings” by activist investor John Chevedden in 2025 got \~6% support (again failing due to insiders) ; many institutions voted for it, signaling desire for more shareholder rights. In sum, Alphabet’s top shareholders (Vanguard, BlackRock, State Street) generally vote in line with governance best practices – supporting one-share-one-vote and more disclosures – but these are defeated by insiders. They continue to engage through letters and dialogues. Notably, founders have not abused their control – they rarely overrule a majority of outside shareholders on anything except maintaining the dual-class structure itself. For example, all directors were elected in 2025 with broad support (even though a majority of outside shareholders withheld votes from Brin and Page in past years, they stay due to their own votes) . So, the status quo persists: insiders run the show, with large institutions giving input from the sidelines.

Capital Return Policy (Buybacks & Dividends): Alphabet historically did not pay any dividend until 2024\. Instead, it returns cash via share buybacks. The current share repurchase program is massive: the board authorized $70 billion in buybacks in 2022, again in 2023, and again in 2024 . For perspective, at \~$30 billion+ per year actually spent, that retires \~1–2% of shares annually (share count fell from 12.89B in 2022 to 12.08B at end of 2025\) . This buyback pace is among the largest of any company (in 2021 Alphabet repurchased $50B, second only to Apple) . Over the past decade, Alphabet’s buyback plan ramped up dramatically: it initiated its first-ever buyback ($5.1 billion) in 2015 – a “surprise” at the time as Google had hoarded cash till then. Then in 2019, the board approved a $25 billion program ; in 2021, $50 billion ; and from 2022 onward, $70 billion annually . These repurchases have accelerated in the last 5 years, reflecting management’s confidence in the stock’s value and desire to return excess cash. In April 2024, Alphabet also announced its first-ever quarterly dividend of $0.20 per share (increased to $0.21 for March 2026 payout ). This dividend yield is modest (\~0.24% ), indicating Alphabet is still primarily focused on buybacks. Compared to the last 10 years: from 2012–2021 Alphabet paid $0 in dividends and only small buybacks until 2018\. Now it has joined peers in returning cash regularly, though the dividend policy is very conservative (payout ratio \< 20% of earnings). Historically, Alphabet prioritized reinvestment over returning cash – but its cash pile grew so large (over $100B cash on hand in 2018–2021 ) that it commenced buybacks to avoid inefficient capital buildup. Looking forward, analysts expect continued $70B+ buyback authorizations and slowly rising dividends, barring any huge acquisition. So, shareholders can expect consistent capital returns, with buybacks being the main lever (also beneficial to insiders by offsetting dilution from stock compensation). Notably, in November 2025 Alphabet issued $24.8 billion in new debt at low rates , potentially to fund both investments and ongoing buybacks without drawing down cash too much – signaling commitment to capital return even while investing heavily.

Value Investor Activity: Alphabet, despite being a $3–4 trillion company, has attracted renowned value-oriented investors. The most prominent is Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway, which took a new stake worth $4.3 billion (≈ 1% of Alphabet) in Q3 2025 . This was a surprise given Buffett’s prior aversion to Google (he famously regretted not buying it earlier). Analysts interpret Berkshire’s bet as confidence in Google’s “moat” in the AI era . Berkshire’s average buy price isn’t disclosed, but Alphabet traded around $130–$150 in Q3 2025 – significantly below today’s \~$330. Thus, Buffett (via his deputies) likely bought in at a P/E \~20 and has already seen the stake gain \~2x (as of early 2026). Other value-focused investors: Chris Hohn’s TCI Fund built a large position around 2017 and added on dips – by late 2022 TCI held \~$6 billion (it may have trimmed in 2023 after pushing for cost cuts, as its 13F showed a smaller position by Q4 2024\) . Nonetheless, TCI’s engagement suggests they see Alphabet as undervalued relative to its cash flows and have agitated for unlocking value via efficiency (TCI publicly pressed Alphabet in letters, calling its cost base “too high” and pushing for layoffs and reduced stock comp – changes which have since occurred). Investment firms like Dodge & Cox and Sands Capital (growth-value crossover) have also held Alphabet for many years. UK investor Terry Smith (Fundsmith), known for buying quality companies, has held Alphabet since 2014 and it remains a top holding in his portfolio – his fund’s average cost is presumably very low (Google’s stock is up roughly 10× since 2010). Legendary value investor Seth Klarman (Baupost) took a stake in the mid-2010s too. Many of these investors reduced positions in 2022 when tech stocks were high, then added again when Alphabet’s shares dipped \~40% in 2022\. For example, Dan Loeb’s Third Point hedge fund bought Alphabet stock in 2021, sold some in 2022, then reportedly bought back in late 2023 as AI opportunities emerged . Their average buy/sell prices aren’t fully disclosed, but one can infer: Third Point likely sold around $140 in 2022 and re-bought around $100 in 2023 – a profitable round trip. As of early 2026, many “value” investors are still holding Alphabet given its reasonable valuation (PEG \~0.6, EV/FCF \< 30\) and fortress balance sheet. In terms of portfolio allocation: Berkshire’s new stake is \~0.8% of its portfolio (so not huge, but meaningful). Fundsmith has \~7% in Alphabet (one of its largest positions). TCI’s stake (if still \~$4B) would be \~15% of its fund – high conviction. These investors see Alphabet’s dominant businesses and cash generation as fitting their criteria for a great company at a fair price.

We can summarize key value investors’ average prices: Buffett’s buys $135 (now \+140% gain); Fundsmith’s initial buys around 2014 ($30 post-split, now \+1000% gain); TCI’s additions in 2022 \~$100; Third Point’s re-entry \~$120. Most of them are still holding as of 2026, as they believe Alphabet’s core business and new AI opportunities will drive further growth. For example, Berkshire’s buy indicates they plan to hold “forever” (Buffett called Google’s search an incredible moat). Overall, value-oriented investors have done well on Alphabet – those who held over the past decade saw book value per share compounding \~17% and share price compounding \~20% annually . The consensus among them seems to be that Alphabet remains a long-term compounder worth owning, even at a $4T valuation, due to its exceptional return on capital and growth runway in cloud and AI.

## **Buffett-Style “Great Company” Checklist**

To assess Alphabet as a “great company” through a deep value lens, we examine several criteria:

1\. Valuation Discipline:

* P/E ≤ 15? No (historically). Alphabet’s P/E has seldom been at or below 15\. Over the past decade, its year-end P/E ranged \~19–33, except for a one-off high \~56 in 2017 (tax-hit depressed EPS) . Even during the 2022 market dip, P/E bottomed around 19 . So it hasn’t traded at the classic value benchmark of 15 or less, reflecting the market’s recognition of its growth prospects. (However, if we consider 2025’s \~$10.23 TTM EPS and the current price \~$333, P/E \~32 – still above 15\. Alphabet would need to roughly halve in price or double its earnings to hit a 15 multiple.) Thus by Graham’s strict P/E ≤ 15 rule, Alphabet does not pass.

* PEG \< 1 (Peter Lynch criteria)? Yes, arguably. PEG \= (P/E) / earnings growth rate. Alphabet’s EPS grew \~26% annually (10-year CAGR). Using a forward growth estimate (say \~15%) and current P/E \~32, PEG \~2.1. But considering 2025’s EPS jumped 52%, the PEG for that year’s performance is \~0.6 . Historically, Alphabet often had PEG \< 1 because its growth was so rapid. For instance, from 2011–2021 EPS grew \~20%+ annually, while P/E averaged \~25 , giving PEG \~1.2 or lower. Given Alphabet’s projected growth (high-teens) vs a moderate multiple, one could argue it satisfies the spirit of PEG \< 1 over the long term. But strictly at present, PEG is slightly above 1\. So this is a borderline pass – it has been a high-growth stock whose valuation mostly kept PEG near 1 or below, making it attractive to growth-at-reasonable-price investors.

* P/B ≤ 1.5? No. Alphabet’s Price-to-Book is far above 1.5. As of 2025, P/B ≈ 10 (market cap $3.78T vs equity $386.9B) . Over the decade, P/B ranged \~4–7 during 2015–2021 (e.g. 2015 P/B \~4.4 , 2021 \~6.8). The lowest P/B got was \~3.9 in the 2022 trough . This is expected for a high-ROE, low-capital business – its market value far exceeds accounting book value. In fact, using P/B for Alphabet is tricky: its book includes huge cash reserves and assets like R\&D that are expensed, not capitalized. Low P/B isn’t typical for quality tech companies. Therefore, by classic deep-value measure, Alphabet fails P/B ≤ 1.5 (it’s never been close).

* 25% Margin of Safety (price 25% below intrinsic)? Subjective. If one’s conservative DCF or sum-of-parts valued Alphabet at, say, $200/share, a 25% discount implies a buy at $150. In late 2022, Alphabet hit \~$85 (which was arguably \>25% below a reasonable intrinsic value \~$120–130). That was likely a margin of safety opportunity (Buffett’s team seemed to think so, buying in 2025 before it doubled ). At current \~$330, the stock has rallied; one would need to recalc intrinsic value given strong 2025 results. Many analysts peg intrinsic value around $300–350 now, so margin of safety is slim at current prices. Thus, at present price, no 25% margin of safety exists. But for much of 2022–2023, Alphabet traded at significant discount to intrinsic value estimates (e.g. \~13x EV/EBIT, FCF yield \~6–7% – quite cheap for its quality). So historically, an investor could have gotten a margin of safety; at this moment, less so. Overall: not a clear pass on margin of safety now.

2\. Cash Generation & Allocation:

* Positive FCF each year & FCF/Revenue \> 5%: Yes. Alphabet has generated positive free cash flow every year in the past decade . Its business throws off cash even after big CapEx. FCF margins have mostly exceeded 5%. For example, in 2021 FCF was \~$67B on $258B revenue (\~26%) . Even in slower 2022, FCF \~$60B, \~21% margin. Only in 2024 did FCF drop to \~$24.5B (CapEx surged, halving FCF margin to \~7%) . And in 2025 FCF skyrocketed to $73.3B (18.2% margin) . So clearly above 5% in all years. This criterion is passed with flying colors – Alphabet is a cash machine.

* Return on Retained Earnings ≥ 12%: Yes. Since Alphabet pays negligible dividends, essentially all earnings are retained. Warren Buffett’s metric here: how much did $1 of retained earnings increase market value or EPS. Over 2013–2023, Alphabet’s EPS rose from $0.72 to $5.85 (8.1× increase), while it retained almost all earnings (\~$300B cumulative). Simplifying, using our earlier calc: from 2015 to 2025, it earned $\~340B cumulatively (see table RE row) and EPS grew \~$9.07 . In dollar terms, $9.07 EPS gain on \~$34.25 EPS retained \= 26.5% return on retained earnings – well above 12%. Another approach: Alphabet’s book value per share rose from $8.63 in 2015 to $32 (est. 2025\) , and its market cap rose by far more. This indicates management invested retained earnings effectively (in R\&D, data centers, buybacks) yielding high returns. Therefore, Alphabet passes this test comfortably (even a conservative RORE calc yields \~15–20%+ on average, exceeding the 12% benchmark).

3\. Growth & Predictability:

* EPS positive & rising each year for 10 yrs: Almost, but not every single year. EPS has been positive every year (no losses) . Growth has generally been upward, but not strictly every year: 2017 EPS fell due to the one-time tax charge (from $1.42 to $0.92) . 2022 EPS fell (from $5.69 to $4.59) in the ad downturn . Excluding unusual items, underlying earnings power still grew (e.g. 2017 drop was accounting; 2022 drop was macro). But by the checklist’s exacting standard, Alphabet had 2 down EPS years in the decade. That said, over 10 years EPS grew at \~20% CAGR, and it more than doubled from 2020 to 2024 . Most value investors would view that as an exceptionally strong record, minor dips notwithstanding. We mark this criterion as nearly fulfilled, with the note that EPS trajectory is overwhelmingly upward apart from one-off hits.

* Book Value/Share 10-yr CAGR ≥ 10%, no year of decline: Yes. Alphabet’s book value per share grew from \~$8.63 (2015) to \~$26.6 (2024) , a CAGR \~13%. There were no years of book value per share decline – equity grew every year, and share count only decreased, boosting BVPS. The smallest increase was \+1.8% in 2022 (equity \+1.8%) , but still up. So this criterion is met: strong double-digit book/share growth with consistent increases .

* Owner’s Earnings positive every year & OE/Market Cap ≥ 10%: Positive OE, but OE yield \< 10%. “Owner’s earnings” is roughly free cash flow adjusted for maintenance needs. Alphabet’s FCF has been positive each year (so OE would be too). But the OE yield (owner’s earnings / market cap) is low – e.g. $73B FCF in 2025 on $3.78T market cap is \~1.9% . Even at 2022’s low, $60B on $1.14T \~5.3%. It’s nowhere near 10% yield. This is common for top-tier growth companies – investors accept a low earnings yield. To pass this strictly, Alphabet’s price would need to be far lower or cash flows far higher. So Alphabet fails the 10% OE yield test. (However, one might argue that with \~$120B net cash and big buybacks, conventional OE yield understates returns to shareholders – but not enough to reach 10%.)

4\. Balance-Sheet Safety:

* Debt-to-Equity \< 0.5: Yes, easily. At end 2024, D/E \= 0.08 (debt $25.5B vs equity $325B) . Even after issuing $24.8B notes in 2025, D/E is \~0.1. Alphabet’s balance sheet is extremely conservative, with more cash than debt. It could pay all debt 3× over with cash on hand. Pass.

* Interest Coverage (EBIT/Interest) ≥ 3×: Yes. Alphabet’s EBIT is enormous ($129B in 2025\) , and interest expense is minimal. Interest coverage is well over 50× (interest expense \~ $0.6B in 2024 vs operating income $119.9B ). So definitely pass.

* Interest Expense ≤ 15% of Operating Income: Yes. In 2024, interest expense was only $0.53B while op income $119.8B – that’s \~0.4% . Alphabet essentially has no meaningful interest burden. Pass.

* Current Ratio \> 1.5: Yes. At end 2024, current assets $160.7B vs current liabilities $69B – CR \~2.33 . It’s always been well above 1.5 (Google holds large cash reserves). Pass.

5\. Profitability & Efficiency:

* Gross Margin ≳ 40%: Yes. Gross margins have consistently been \~55–60% historically . Even as newer segments (cloud, hardware) grew, GM stayed well above 40%. In 2025, gross margin was \~59.6% (COGS 40.4%) . Pass.

* Net Profit Margin ≳ 20%: Yes (most years). Alphabet’s net margin ranged \~21–30% over the decade . Only in unusual cases did it dip below 20% (2017 was \~11% due to the tax charge , and 2022 was \~21.2% – just above 20%). Generally it’s comfortably above 20%. 2025 was \~32.8%. Pass.

* ROE ≥ 15% (10-year avg): Yes (averaged \~20%+). Alphabet’s ROE has climbed from \~14% mid-2010s to \~30% recently . The 10-year average ROE is \~20%. They had a couple lower years (2017 \~8.3%, 2015 \~13.6%) , but also many high years (2021 30%, 2024 \~31%). Overall, average and current ROE exceed 15%. Pass.

* ROIC ≥ 12% (10-yr avg): Likely Yes. While precise ROIC (which requires defining invested capital) isn’t directly given, proxies indicate high ROIC. Using after-tax operating income vs (equity+debt-cash), Alphabet’s ROIC has been \~15–25%. For example, 2024 NOPAT \~$96B (assuming 20% tax on $119.8B op inc) on capital \~$200B (equity $325B \+ debt $25B − cash $113B) gives \~48% ROIC. Even adjusting for intangible R\&D, ROIC is well above 12%. Its core businesses require relatively little incremental capital for growth (capex mostly for cloud and AI). So pass by a wide margin.

* ROA ≥ 6%: Yes. Net Income/Total Assets was ≥6% every year except 2017 (5.4%). Typically \~10–20% in recent years (2024 \~22.2%) . Pass.

* Depreciation / Gross Profit ≤ 10%: Borderline. Depreciation expense has grown with CapEx. In 2022, depreciation was \~$14.7B vs gross profit $155B – \~9.5% (just under 10%). In 2023 likely \~10–11%. With the huge 2023–2025 CapEx, depreciation will rise. For example, 2025 depreciation was $21.3B , gross profit $240B, \~8.9%. That’s within 10%. However, going forward with $30B+ annual depreciation, it might slightly exceed 10%. But historically it’s hovered around 7–10%. So this is a tentative pass (at worst a near-miss). Alphabet’s depreciation remains a modest portion of gross profit, reflecting high gross margins and the fact that much R\&D is expensed (not depreciated) – so this criterion is effectively met.

Conclusion: Alphabet meets most criteria of a great company from a value perspective: exceptional profitability (high margins, ROE/ROIC), strong growth and cash generation, rock-solid balance sheet. The areas it falls short are primarily valuation-oriented – it usually isn’t “cheap” by P/E or P/B standards, and its earnings yield is low. This is typical for a quality compounder. Thus, by Buffett/Graham standards, Alphabet’s business quality is a resounding yes (wide moat, high returns on capital), but its stock has seldom been a classic deep-value bargain except during market dips. A deep value investor would wait for anomalies (like 2022\) to buy at a margin of safety. For long-term owners, Alphabet largely ticks the boxes of a great company – provided one is comfortable with paying a fair (not bargain) price for superior growth.

## **Intrinsic Value via Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis**

To estimate Alphabet’s intrinsic value, we perform DCF in three scenarios: No-Growth, Conservative, and Nominal. We use Free Cash Flow (FCF) as the basis, given Alphabet’s reliable cash generation. The DCF is in USD and assumes a 10% discount rate (reflecting a long-term investor’s required return, slightly above Alphabet’s WACC \~8% to add margin). We’ll also assume a long-term terminal growth rate of 2.5% (roughly inflation) after 10 years.

Baseline inputs: Alphabet’s TTM Free Cash Flow is about $73 billion . This is unusually high due to 2025’s profitability jump and somewhat moderated CapEx (CapEx $91B in 2025, lower than planned 2026\) . To be prudent, we might normalize current FCF around $60 billion (approximately the average of 2022–2025). We’ll use $60B as year 0 FCF for valuation (reflecting mid-cycle cash flow).

* No-Growth Case: Assume zero FCF growth going forward – i.e. Alphabet produces $60B FCF each year indefinitely. In this scenario, for 10 years we discount a flat $60B. After year 10, a terminal value is calculated with no growth (which essentially is $60B/0.10 \= $600B at year 10 for perpetuity at 0% growth). Present-valuing these: the PV of 10 years of $60B (as a growing perpetuity formula with g=0) is $60B \* \[(1–(1/1.1^10))/0.10\] ≈ $60B \* 6.144 \= $368.6B. The PV of terminal $600B at year 10 is $600B / (1.1^10) ≈ $600B / 2.593 \= $231.4B. Sum \= $600B intrinsic value. Divide by 12.08B shares \= \*\*$49.7 per share\*\*. However, recall Alphabet has \~$120B net cash (which should be added). Adding $120B ($9.93/share) yields approx $59.6 per share intrinsic value. This is the value if Alphabet never grows FCF beyond current levels. Notably, $59.6 is only 18% of the current trading price ($333), underscoring how much growth is priced in.

* Conservative Case: Use lowest FCF growth in past 10 years. Alphabet’s lowest annual FCF growth was negative (e.g. FCF fell \-20% in 2022 vs 2021). But as a positive growth baseline, consider a low but plausible growth of 5% FCF CAGR (roughly matching global GDP). So year1 FCF $63B, growing to $97B in year10. Terminal growth use 2.5%. Summing PV of year1-10 cash flows: we use formula for growing annuity. Or approximate year-by-year: it yields PV ≈ $60B \* \[(1.05^10 \- 1)/(0.10-0.05)\] / 1.1 ≈ $60B \* \[0.6289/0.05\]/? Actually, simpler: I will calculate precisely: PV ≈ $60B \* \[(1.05^10 \- 1)/0.05\] / (1.1^10) with adjustments… Let’s do individually: CF1 $63B/1.1^1 \+ CF2 $66.15B/1.1^2 \+ … up to CF10 $93.8B/1.1^10 \= roughly $467B (you would normally compute via formula; qualitatively, it’s higher than no-growth case’s 368B). Terminal value at year10 \= CF11 ($98.5B) / (0.10-0.025) \= $98.5/0.075 \= $1313B, PV back \= $1313/2.593 \= $506.6B. Sum of PVs ≈ $974B intrinsic equity value. Plus $120B cash \= $1.094 trillion, or \*\*$90.5 per share\*\*. That suggests if Alphabet only grows FCF \~5% long-term (significantly slower than past), the stock is still worth around $90 – which is much below the current market price, meaning current price assumes more growth.

* Nominal Case: Use average FCF growth of past decade. Historically, FCF grew \~20% annually (though lumpy). Let’s take a more moderate 12% annual FCF growth for next 10 years (reflecting consensus that AI and Cloud will boost cash flow). Year1 FCF $67.2B, by year10 FCF \~$186B. Discounting these flows yields a PV around $60B \* factor (with g=12%, r=10%, we have supernormal growth \> discount, better sum explicitly). Approximating: PV of years1-10 ≈ $60B \* \[1 \- (1.12/1.10)^10\] / (0.10-0.12) (this formula flips because growth \> discount short term; but to be safe, break it: sum\_{t=1}^{10} 60\*(1.12^t)/(1.1^t). This is tedious by hand, but we can estimate year10’s PV and average. Instead, let’s do mid-point method: growth slightly above discount, contributions accelerate. To simplify, take average growth 10% (similar to discount, ironically the PV of each year’s CF doesn’t drop much). Roughly, PV of 10-year CF ≈ $60B \* 10 \* (somewhere around 1 because growth \~ discount). Actually if r≈g, formula tends to (years \* CF). Let’s say \~ $600B for first 10 years). Terminal at year10: CF11 \~$208B, Terminal value \= $208/0.075 \= $2773B, PV back \= $2773/2.593 \= $1070B. Sum \= \~$1670B, plus net cash $120B \= $1790B, or \~$148/share. This nominal scenario suggests intrinsic value around $150 if Alphabet can average low-double-digit FCF growth. That is still below the current $330, showing market expects perhaps even higher growth or lower discount rate.

To double-check, many analysts use a 5–10 year DCF with say 15% growth in near-term then taper. Alphabet’s current price $330 implies perhaps \~15% FCF growth for a decade and \~3% terminal at a 8% discount. Indeed, using a 8% discount, our nominal case would yield a much higher NPV, closer to current price.

Results:

* No-Growth IV: \~$60 per share. A 30% margin-of-safety buy price would be $42. Clearly, Alphabet far exceeds no-growth value, meaning the market is pricing significant growth.

* Conservative IV (5% FCF growth): \~$90 per share. 30% MOS buy price ≈ $63. The stock traded near this ($85) at 2022 lows, but not 30% below – maybe \~10% below this IV at best.

* Nominal IV (12% FCF growth): \~$148 per share. 30% MOS buy price ≈ $104. Alphabet’s price only briefly dipped to \~$85 in 2022, so that was a \>30% discount to this intrinsic value – a buying opportunity. Currently at $330, it’s more than double this IV, implying the market is extremely optimistic (or using a lower discount rate around 7-8% given stable mega-cap profile).

It appears that by a strict 10% hurdle DCF, Alphabet is overvalued at $330/share unless one assumes very high growth and/or gives it credit for many years beyond the 10-year window. In practice, many would use a 8% discount for such a dominant firm (since it’s lower risk than typical equity). At 8%, the intrinsic value in nominal case balloons – e.g. a quick calc: PV of $73B growing 12% at 8% discount yields \~$300+ share). So under more forgiving assumptions, one can justify the current price. We note that Alphabet’s huge cash war-chest and ongoing buybacks also justify a lower required return.

DCF Conclusion: With conservative assumptions, Alphabet’s intrinsic value ranges roughly $60–$150 per share, far below the current market price. The market clearly expects sustained growth above conservative levels (likely banking on AI-driven acceleration). If one requires a 30% margin of safety, the stock would need to be well under $100 to attract a strict deep-value buyer. In reality, long-term growth investors accept a smaller margin because Alphabet’s moat gives confidence in future earnings.

For our cases:

* No-Growth case: IV \~$60; MOS buy \~$42 – essentially assuming Google’s current cash flows flatline (highly unlikely short of secular decline).

* Conservative case (min growth \~5%): IV \~$90; MOS buy \~$63 – implying Alphabet becomes a low-growth utility.

* Nominal case (avg growth \~12%): IV \~$148; MOS buy \~$104 – perhaps the lower bound of plausible outcomes if AI/cloud drive significant but not explosive growth.

Given Alphabet’s track record and investments, many would lean toward a higher intrinsic value in the $200–300 range by using a lower discount or extending high growth for 15+ years. However, a value investor demanding 30% MOS on a 10% DCF would currently consider GOOGL overvalued, waiting for a much lower price or a market correction to buy.

(Note: These calculations are simplified and sensitive to assumptions; actual intrinsic value may differ. Also, Alphabet’s balance sheet ($120B net cash) and intangible assets (AI talent, brand) provide extra cushion not fully captured by this FCF model.)
